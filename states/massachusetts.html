How Massachusetts Energy Works

What Deregulation Means
Massachusetts deregulated its electricity market in 1998 under the Electric Restructuring Act, allowing businesses to choose their retail electricity supplier instead of paying the utility's default Basic Service rate. Your distribution infrastructure, metering, and emergency response all remain exactly the same — you simply choose who bills you for power generation, and at what fixed price.
In effect since 1998
⚡
Why Massachusetts Is Different
Massachusetts operates within ISO-NE (Independent System Operator New England) — a competitive regional wholesale electricity market. The state's three major utilities — Eversource Energy (eastern MA), National Grid (central and western MA), and Unitil (northeastern MA) — all operate in a fully open retail market. Massachusetts businesses face some of the highest commercial electricity rates in the country, making the savings from a competitive fixed-rate contract particularly significant.
ISO-NE — New England competitive grid
💰
What This Means for Your Business
Most Massachusetts businesses remain on the utility's Basic Service rate — an adjustable rate updated every six months that tracks regional wholesale prices and incorporates the utility's full risk margin. When New England wholesale prices spike during winter cold snaps or summer heat waves, that cost lands directly on your bill. A fixed-rate contract eliminates that exposure entirely.
