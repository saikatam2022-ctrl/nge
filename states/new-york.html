What Deregulation Means
New York deregulated its electricity market in 1996 under the Competitive Opportunities proceeding of the New York Public Service Commission, making it one of the earliest states to open electricity generation to competition. Businesses across all Con Edison, National Grid, NYSEG, Central Hudson, Orange & Rockland, and RG&E territories can choose their retail electricity supplier, paying the utility only for distribution. Your wires, reliability, and emergency service remain unchanged.
In effect since 1996
⚡
Why New York Is Different
New York operates within NYISO (New York Independent System Operator) — a sophisticated competitive wholesale electricity market divided into eleven load zones. New York City and Long Island (Con Edison and PSEG-LI territory) face the highest commercial rates in the state, driven by transmission constraints and dense urban demand. Upstate markets under National Grid, NYSEG, and RG&E benefit from proximity to hydroelectric generation but still carry significant savings potential through competitive fixed-rate contracts.

What This Means for Your Business
Most New York businesses roll onto their utility's default Supply Service rate — a monthly-adjusted price that incorporates the utility's full hedging costs and administrative margin. New York City commercial accounts face some of the highest electricity prices in the continental US. A fixed-rate contract from a licensed competitive supplier locks in below-market pricing and eliminates monthly rate variability. Our free audit identifies your exact overpayment in under 10 minutes.
