How Maryland Energy Works
Why Maryland businesses have more power than they realise

🏛️
What Deregulation Means
Maryland deregulated its electricity market in 2000 under the Electric Customer Choice and Competition Act of 1999, allowing businesses to choose their retail electricity supplier instead of paying their utility's default Standard Offer Service rate. Your distribution wires, metering, and emergency response all remain the same — you simply choose who supplies your generation, and at what price.
In effect since 2000
⚡
Why Maryland Is Different
Maryland operates within the PJM Interconnection — the world's largest competitive wholesale electricity market. The state is served by four major utilities: BGE (Baltimore Gas and Electric) serving Baltimore and central Maryland, Pepco serving the DC suburbs of Prince George's and Montgomery counties, Delmarva Power serving the Eastern Shore, and Potomac Edison serving western Maryland. Each territory has access to a full pool of competing retail suppliers.

What This Means for Your Business
Most Maryland businesses default to their utility's Standard Offer Service — a rate that tracks wholesale market prices and spikes during summer peak demand periods. Maryland's dense concentration of government contractors, biotech firms, and professional services means many commercial accounts carry significant, predictable loads that are ideal for a fixed-rate contract.
