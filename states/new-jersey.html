How New Jersey Energy Works
Why New Jersey businesses have more power than they realise
New Jersey deregulated its electricity market in 1999. That single change means your business can shop its energy rate — and most businesses simply haven't.
🏛️
What Deregulation Means
New Jersey deregulated its electricity market in 1999 under the Electric Discount and Energy Competition Act (EDECA), giving businesses the right to choose a competitive retail electricity supplier (ARES) rather than defaulting to the utility's Price to Compare (PTC). Your distribution wires, grid reliability, and emergency response remain exactly the same — you simply choose who supplies your generation, and at what fixed price.
In effect since 1999
⚡
Why New Jersey Is Different
New Jersey is one of the highest-rate states in the northeast — which means the spread between the utility's default rate and a competitively bid fixed rate is larger than almost anywhere else. The state sits within the PJM Interconnection, and businesses in PSE&G (northern and central NJ), JCP&L (central and northern NJ), Atlantic City Electric (southern NJ), and Rockland Electric (Bergen County) territories can all access a wide pool of competing suppliers.
PJM — world's largest competitive grid
💰
What This Means for Your Business
Most New Jersey businesses roll onto the utility's Price to Compare (PTC) — an energy rate that adjusts semi-annually based on wholesale procurement auctions. Those auctions include overhead, hedging costs, and a margin that you pay for simply by staying on the default. A competitively bid fixed-rate contract strips all of that out. New Jersey's high baseline rates mean the dollar savings per kWh are among the largest of any deregulated state. Our free audit shows you exactly how much you're overpaying.
